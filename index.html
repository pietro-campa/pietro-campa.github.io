As an applied microeconomist, my research centers on the intergenerational transmission of inequalities. I am particularly interested in the effects of sorting across neighborhoods and schools, and how early interactions shape access to opportunities through educational outcomes and social networks within the labor market.

Job Market Paper

"No Kid is an Island: Peer Effects and Intergenerational Mobility"

This paper explores the relevance of peer effects on intergenerational mobility by examining how exogenous exposure to high school peers’ from higher-earning families influences access to opportunities. Using comprehensive administrative data from Denmark, I show that a one standard deviation increase in average schoolmates’ parental earnings leads to a 2% increase in lifetime earnings. Leveraging variation in former schoolmates’ initial coworkers’ wage trajectories, I identify endogenous peer affects accounting for 30% of the exposure effect via former schoolmates acting as weak ties on the labor market. This paper highlights the role of social interactions in reinforcing the persistence of economic outcomes from one generation to the next.
